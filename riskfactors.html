Background

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is the most common systemic inflammatory rheumatic disease. It is associated with significant burden at the patient and societal level. RA is a multifactorial disease, wherein complex interactions between host and environmental factors determine the overall risk of disease susceptibility, persistence and severity. Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic immune-mediated multisystemic disease that mainly localizes to the joints. It is a systemic inflammatory rheumatic disease (2,3), associated with considerable morbidity and disability, as well as increased mortality RA is a multifactorial disease, with a complex interplay between the host and the environment determining the overall risk of disease susceptibility, persistence and severity (4,5). Risk factors for developing RA can be generically divided into host- and environment-related (figure) . Host factors that have been associated with RA development may be further grouped into genetic; epigenetic; hormonal, reproductive and neuroendocrine; and comorbid host factors. In turn, environmental risk factors include smoking and other airborne exposures; microbiota and infectious agents; diet; and socioeconomic factors.

1. HOST FACTORS

a) Genetic Factors

Genetic Factor Details
Over 100 loci Associated with increased RA risk in trans-ethnic populations. (6,7)
Major histocompatibility complex (MHC) locus First to be identified and remains the most studied region, accounting for around a third of the disease genetic susceptibility.(8)
Human leukocyte antigen (HLA) loci HLA-DRB1 strongly associated with RA in most populations. Risk varies according to specific alleles and ancestry.(9)
Shared epitope (SE) Sequence of five amino acids in residues 70–74 in the DRß1-chain, associated with ACPA-positive RA.(9-13)
Non-HLA genes More than 100 risk loci validated across multiple populations, most related to immune cell function.(6,7,14,15)
SNP in the PTPN22 gene Encodes a protein tyrosine phosphatase involved in antigen receptor signaling of B and T cells, associated with seropositive RA.(16)
Non-HLA Genetic Variants Variants in genes like CTLA4, STAT4, TNFAIP3, TRAF1-C5, IL2/21, CD40, IL2RA/IL2RB, IL6R, and RNASET2 associated with RA.(17-26)

b) Epigenetic Factors

Epigenetic Factor Details
DNA Methylation Differential DNA methylation signatures in Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells and Fibro-blast Like Synoviocytes of RA patients, including hypo- or hypermethylation of specific promoter regions, which lead to an increase or decrease, respectively, in the transcription of pro-(9e.g. IL-6,IL-6R,CXCL12,CD40L) or anti-inflammatory[e.g.,CTLA4 in Treg cell] genes.(27-30)
Histone Modification Increased expression of histone deacetylases (HDAC) in RA synovial tissue, HDAC inhibitors suppress inflammation[IL-6 and type-I interferon(IFN) responses].(31-36)
Non-coding RNA Includes microRNAs (miRNAs like miRNA-155, miRNA-146a, miRNA-223 and miRNA124a) and long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs like HOTAIR) that regulate gene transcription and are differentially expressed in RA.(32,37-44)

c) Hormonal, Reproductive and Neuroendocrine Factors

Factor Details
Hormonal Effects Anti-inflammatory effects of progesterone, estrogens and androgens decreased in RA patients.(45,46)
Reproductive Factors Factors like breastfeeding, pregnancy loss, early menarche, age at first pregnancy, OCs, and hormone replacement therapy influence development of RA.(47,48)
Situations associated with decline in estrogen load Such as early menopause, post-menopausal stage, puerperium and anti-estrogen agents and aromatase inhibitors have been identified as risk factors of RA(49-53)
Pre-eclampsia Fetal Microchimerism [exchange and persistence of fetal cells in maternal circulation] increased in women with RA and pre-eclampsia, it is thought to mediate maternal acquisition of the SE (54-56)
Birth Weight Contribute to RA through downregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal(HPA) axis leading to decreased cortisol levels and responsiveness.(57-59)
Neuroendocrine System Disturbances in the neuroendocrine system and its relation with the immune system have been implicated in RA(60,61)
Stress Effect of psychological stress, including childhood traumatic stress and post-traumatic stress disorder leads to RA(62-67)
Vitamin D Endocrine mediator with immunomodulatory properties, reduced vitamin D levels common in RA patients.(68,69)
Obesity Positive association with RA, stronger in women and potentially seronegative disease.(70,71)

d) Comorbid Host Factors

Comorbid Factor Details
Psychiatric Conditions Conditions like PTSD, depression, and schizophrenia increase RA risk.(64,65,67,72-79)
Atopy and Allergic Diseases Includes asthma, allergic rhinitis, atopic dermatitis, related to Th2-dependent pathways.(80-84)
Respiratory Diseases Both acute and chronic respiratory diseases associated with increased RA risk.(83,85-88)
Non-rheumatological Immune-mediated Diseases Includes autoimmune thyroid disease, type 1 diabetes mellitus, alopecia areata, vitiligo, inflammatory bowel disease, and multiple sclerosis.(89-95)
Sleep Disorders Disorders like obstructive sleep apnea and non-apnea sleep disorders linked to increased RA risk.(96-99)

2. ENVIRONMENTAL FACTORS

a) Smoking and Other Airborne Exposures

Exposure Details
Smoking Established as a major risk factor, contributes to 20-25% of overall RA risk, especially ACPA-positive RA(35%). Responsible for insitu protein citrullination (conversion of arginine to citrulline) in the lungs(100-105)
Passive Smoking Possible link with parental, childhood and adult passive exposure to cigarette smoke can increase risk of RA (106-110)
Silica Common occupational exposure associated with RA, especially ACPA-positive RA, with additive effect when combined with smoking.(111-114)
Textile Dust Associated with RA, interaction with HLA-SE presence increases risk.(115)
Inorganic Dust Asbestos, Cement were also linked to RA(116)
Ambient Air Pollution Not consistently linked to RA risk.(117)

b) Microbiota and Infectious Agents

Agent Details
Periodontitis Results from dysbiosis of the oral microbiota and has been associated with increased RA risk, bidirectional relationship, shared genetic and environmental risk factors.(118,119)
Periodontitis Agents Porphyromonas gingivalis is the major cause of periodontitis, associated with RA through chronic citrullination of proteins.(120). Aggregatibacter actinomycetemcomitans directly implicated in RA through different mechanisms involving neutrophil-mediated citullination.(121)
Gut Microbiota Intestinal dysbiosis linked to RA, with decreased microbial diversity (Bacteroides spp. and Haemophilus spp.) and specific bacterial enrichment (Prevotella copri(can generate RA-specific Th1 and Th17 responses), Lactobacillus spp. and Clostridium spp.).(122-129)
External Infectious Agents Common agents include Epstein-Barr virus(EBV), cytomegalovirus(CMV), parvovirus B19, rubella virus, mycoplasma,Proteus mirabilis, Escherichia coli, hepatitis B/C virus, Borrelia burdoferi(Lyme's disease), Chiikungunya virus, and others linked through molecular mimicry and other mechanisms.(130-132)

c) Diet

Dietary Factor Details
Dietary Patterns Adherence to healthy dietary patterns like the Mediterranean diet inversely associated with RA risk.(133)
Individual Dietary Components Omega-3 fatty acids such as olive oil and fish associated with reduced risk; excess sodium and sugar with increased risk.(134-139)
Coffee,Tea And Caffeine Inconsistently associated with RA. One study shows, an increased rish with total coffee consumption and no association with tea consumption(140). Other study shows, no increased rish with coffee (caffeinated or decaffeinated) consumption, but caffeinated tea intake has increased in RA risk. (141)

d) Socioeconomic Factors

Factor Details
Socioeconomic Status (SES) Low status increaes RA risk and worsend outcomes; linked to poor education and early-life factors.(142-144)
Deprivation Associated with unmeasured exposures (e.g., infections, poor diet) and blue-collar jobs.
Blue Collor Jobs Exposure to dust, mineral oil, pesticides, physical workload increases RA risk (ACPA+/ACPA-).(144-148)
Cold Work Environment Raises RA risk by 50%; additive risk with repetitive hand movements.(149)
Work-Related Stress Stress, work conflict, and shift work contribute to increased RA risk.
Physical Activity and BMI Physical activity reduces inflammation and BMI, offering protection against RA.(150,151)
